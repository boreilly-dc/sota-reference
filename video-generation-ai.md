## Executive Summary

Video generation AI in 2026 is the most capital-intensive and fastest-moving corner of generative media. The field has converged on a single dominant architecture — the **latent video Diffusion Transformer (DiT)** — and is now bifurcating along a second axis borrowed directly from large language models: whether video is generated by **denoising continuous latents** (diffusion) or by **predicting discrete tokens one at a time** (autoregression, exactly as an LLM predicts words). The same building blocks that made LLMs work — tokenisers, transformers, scaling laws, and contrastive text embeddings — are what make modern video generation work.

The headline findings:

- **Proprietary frontier** is led by **OpenAI Sora 2**, **Google Veo 3.1**, **Kuaishou Kling 3.0**, and **ByteDance Seedance 2.0**, with **Runway Gen-4.5**, **MiniMax Hailuo**, **Luma Ray3**, **Pika**, and **Adobe Firefly Video** filling specialist niches. The defining 2026 capability is **native synchronised audio** — dialogue, sound effects, and ambience generated in the same pass as the pixels. Veo 3.1 and Seedance 2.0 lead here; Sora 2's audio is more limited. Clip lengths run 5–20 s (up to ~1–2 min on premium tiers), resolutions reach 1080p–4K, and API pricing sits around **US$0.03–0.75 per second** of output.
- **Open-weight** is led by **Alibaba Wan 2.1/2.2** (Apache 2.0), **Tencent HunyuanVideo 1.5** (8.3B, community licence), and **Lightricks LTX-Video** (fast, with a licence trap), alongside **Mochi 1**, **CogVideoX**, **Open-Sora 2.0**, **SkyReels-V2**, and **Step-Video-T2V**. The best open models are genuinely competitive for social/B-roll work and run on a single consumer GPU, but a quality gap to the top proprietary models remains for cinematic, long-form, and audio-native output — and the popular "open model beat Sora on VBench" claim refers to **Sora 1 in early 2025** and is now stale.
- **The LLM/embedding connection** is the through-line. **3D causal VAEs** compress video ~4–8× in time and 8× per spatial dimension into a short latent sequence; **MAGVIT-v2's** lookup-free quantisation and **NVIDIA's Cosmos Tokenizer** turn video into discrete tokens so a decoder-only transformer (**VideoPoet**, **Emu3**, **NOVA**, **Loong**) can "speak video" the way it speaks text. **T5/UMT5 + CLIP** dual text encoders (or, in HunyuanVideo, a Llama-based MLLM) inject the prompt via cross-attention, and contrastive video-text models (**CLIP**, **SigLIP 2**, **InternVideo2**, **VideoPrism**, **LanguageBind**) provide both the conditioning signal and the evaluation metrics (**CLIPSIM**, and the **VBench**/**FVD** benchmark stack).
- **World models** are the frontier's frontier: **Google DeepMind Genie 3** (real-time interactive worlds), **NVIDIA Cosmos** (physical-AI / robotics), **Decart Oasis**, and **World Labs Marble** generate *navigable, controllable* environments rather than fixed clips — pushing video generation toward interactive simulation.

A consistent caution runs through this article: **public leaderboards are volatile and not comparable across snapshots**, many capability claims are **vendor self-reported**, several "open" models carry **non-commercial or custom licences**, and a few cutting-edge announcements (notably NVIDIA Cosmos 3 and some Chinese version bumps) rest on **thin or AI-generated sourcing**. Each is flagged in context.

## The Shared Technical Foundation: VAEs, DiTs, and the LLM Connection

The central problem of video generation is dimensionality. A few seconds of HD video is hundreds of millions of pixels — far too many to model directly. Every modern system therefore works in two stages: a **compressor** that maps pixels to a compact latent representation, and a **generator** (a transformer) that produces those latents from a text prompt. This is structurally identical to how an LLM works on compressed sub-word tokens rather than raw characters — and the parallel runs deep enough that the same architecture, training recipes, and even the same text encoders are shared.

### Video VAEs and Tokenisers

The compressor is almost always a **3D causal Variational Autoencoder (VAE)**. Where an image VAE uses 2D convolutions, a video VAE uses **causal 3D convolutions** that compress along time *and* the two spatial axes simultaneously, while the "causal" constraint ensures each frame depends only on previous frames (so the model can stream and extend video). Typical spatiotemporal compression ratios are **4× temporal × 8× × 8× spatial** (HunyuanVideo, CogVideoX) up to **8×8×8** (NVIDIA Cosmos), with an additional channel expansion. A 5-second clip of millions of pixels collapses to a latent grid small enough for a transformer to handle (sources: [HunyuanVideo](https://github.com/Tencent-Hunyuan/HunyuanVideo); [CogVideoX](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.06072); [Wan 2.1](https://github.com/Wan-Video/Wan2.1)).

There are two flavours of compressor, and the choice determines what kind of generator can sit on top:

| Tokeniser / VAE | Origin | Output type | Compression | Feeds |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **3D Causal VAE** (Wan-VAE, Hunyuan VAE) | Alibaba / Tencent (2024–25) | continuous latents | ~4×8×8 | diffusion DiTs |
| **MAGVIT-v2** | Google (2023) | **discrete tokens** (lookup-free quantisation, 2¹⁸ vocab) | spatiotemporal | autoregressive LLMs (VideoPoet) |
| **Cosmos Tokenizer** | NVIDIA (2025) | both — CV (continuous) and DV (discrete) variants, e.g. CV8×8×8, DV4×8×8 | up to 8×8×8 | diffusion *and* AR |
| **OmniTokenizer** | NeurIPS 2024 | discrete/continuous, transformer-based | arbitrary length/res | joint image+video |

**MAGVIT-v2** was the pivotal result: its **lookup-free quantisation (LFQ)** dropped the traditional vector-quantisation codebook in favour of a binary-decomposed vocabulary of 2¹⁸ (262,144) codes that avoids codebook collapse. The paper's title — *"Language Model Beats Diffusion: Tokenizer is Key to Visual Generation"* — captures the thesis: with a good enough tokeniser, an autoregressive transformer can match or beat diffusion on visual generation. **NVIDIA's Cosmos Tokenizer** generalised this into a suite offering both continuous variants (for diffusion models) and discrete variants (for AR LLMs) of the same compressor, reportedly up to 12× faster than prior methods (sources: [MAGVIT-v2 / ICLR 2024](https://arxiv.org/abs/2310.05737); [Cosmos Tokenizer](https://research.nvidia.com/labs/cosmos-lab/cosmos-tokenizer/); [OmniTokenizer](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.09399)).

### The Diffusion Transformer and the Three Generation Paradigms

On top of the latent space sits the generator. By 2026 the **Diffusion Transformer (DiT)** has displaced the U-Net as the dominant backbone — **Sora, Veo, Wan, HunyuanVideo, and CogVideoX are all DiT-based**. A DiT treats the spatiotemporal latent as a sequence of patches and applies transformer attention to denoise them, which gives the same clean scaling behaviour that made transformers win in language. Three generation paradigms coexist, often blended:

1. **Latent diffusion (DDPM-style)** — iteratively denoise a continuous latent conditioned on the text embedding, then decode to pixels with the VAE. High fidelity; the default for almost all proprietary and open clip-generators.
2. **Flow matching / rectified flow** — instead of DDPM's curved noise schedule, learn a near **straight-line** transport from noise to data. This needs fewer sampling steps and trains more stably; it is the formulation behind **Stable Diffusion 3** and **Flux** and now underpins most modern video DiTs (Wan, LTX-Video, Pyramid Flow). Rectified flow, flow matching, and stochastic interpolants are different names for essentially the same idea.
3. **Autoregressive token prediction** — discretise video with a tokeniser (MAGVIT-v2/Cosmos-DV) and predict tokens one at a time with a decoder-only transformer, exactly like an LLM. Strong temporal coherence and unified multimodal training; historically lower fidelity, bounded by tokeniser quality.

The latent-diffusion pipeline in one line: **encode video → DiT denoises the latent under text conditioning → VAE decodes back to pixels** (sources: [Scaling Rectified Flow Transformers / SD3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.03206); [rectified flow](https://rectifiedflow.github.io/); [Video DiT overview](https://www.emergentmind.com/topics/video-diffusion-transformer-dit)).

### Treating Video as a Language: Autoregressive Video Models

This is the most direct expression of the "video as an LLM problem" idea, and the answer to the brief's emphasis on LLMs. Once a tokeniser turns video into discrete integers, generating video becomes **next-token prediction** — the same objective that trains GPT-style models:

- **VideoPoet** (Google, ICML 2024) is the canonical example: a **decoder-only transformer** that ingests text, image, video, and audio as a single discrete vocabulary (video via MAGVIT-v2, audio via SoundStream), pretrained on **2 trillion multimodal tokens** with standard LLM-style pretraining + task adaptation. It generates video and matching audio from the same model.
- **Emu3** (BAAI, published in *Nature* 2025) pushes the thesis to its limit: a **single transformer trained solely with next-token prediction** over text, image, and video tokens — no diffusion, no CLIP-plus-LLM composition — that matches or beats task-specific models on both generation and perception. *"Next-token prediction is all you need."*
- **NOVA** (BAAI, ICLR 2025) bridges the two worlds: it keeps autoregressive **temporal** ordering (frame-by-frame) and spatial set-by-set prediction, but works in **continuous** space and denoises each prediction with diffusion — avoiding the fidelity loss of vector quantisation while retaining AR's coherence and streaming.
- **Loong** generates **minute-long** video with an AR LLM via progressive short-to-long training, directly confronting the same long-context degradation problem LLMs face with long text.

The practical contrast: **AR models** (VideoPoet, Emu3, Loong) give unified any-to-any multimodal training and strong temporal consistency but have historically trailed on raw visual fidelity; **diffusion models** (Sora, Wan, Hunyuan) lead on fidelity but are harder to unify with text/audio in one network. **NOVA** and Cosmos's mixed architectures show the two paradigms converging (sources: [VideoPoet](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.14125); [Emu3](https://arxiv.org/abs/2409.18869); [NOVA](https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.14169); [Loong](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.02757)).

### Text-Conditioning and Video Embedding Models

The prompt enters the model through a **text encoder**, and the choice mirrors image generation:

- **T5-XXL / UMT5** — instruction-tuned encoder-decoder text models supply deep semantic embeddings. **Wan 2.1** uses **T5-XXL** (UMT5 for multilingual) plus **CLIP ViT-H/14**; this dual-encoder pattern (semantic richness from T5, vision-language grounding from CLIP) is the field default.
- **CLIP / OpenCLIP text encoder** — provides vision-language-aligned embeddings and, for image-to-video, encodes the conditioning image.
- **Llama-based MLLM** — **HunyuanVideo's** notable innovation is replacing T5 with a **multimodal large language model** (a Llama-derived decoder) as the text encoder, for richer semantic guidance.

These embeddings are injected into the DiT via **cross-attention** (text serves as keys/values in the transformer blocks). Variants exist: SD3's **MMDiT** uses bidirectional attention between text and visual tokens with per-modality weights; **CogVideoX** uses an *expert adaptive LayerNorm* to modulate the transformer on text features instead of standard cross-attention.

The same **contrastive video-text embedding models** that condition generation also evaluate it and power retrieval — the embedding layer is simultaneously the input bridge, the search index, and the yardstick. **CLIP** and its successors **SigLIP 2**, **ViCLIP** (CLIP + spatiotemporal attention, from InternVideo), **InternVideo2**, **VideoPrism** (Google's frozen video encoder, SOTA on 31/33 understanding benchmarks), and **LanguageBind** (aligns video/audio/depth/thermal to a shared *language* space) are detailed in [Embedding Models and Evaluation](#embedding-models-and-evaluation). Crucially, these are the **same encoders used as the vision backbone in multimodal LLMs/VLMs** — LanguageBind is the video encoder in Video-LLaVA, InternVideo2 connects to dialogue systems via a next-token-prediction stage — so video understanding, video generation conditioning, and VLMs all draw on one shared family of CLIP-descended encoders (sources: [Wan text/image encoding](https://github.com/Wan-Video/Wan2.1); [HunyuanVideo text encoders](https://github.com/Tencent-Hunyuan/HunyuanVideo); [VideoPrism](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.13217); [LanguageBind](https://github.com/PKU-YuanGroup/LanguageBind)).

### Leaderboards and Best-in-Class by Use Case

The main public preference benchmark is the **Artificial Analysis Video Arena** — a blind-vote, Elo-style leaderboard analogous to LMArena for LLMs. **Treat its absolute rankings with heavy caution**: snapshots taken months apart are **not comparable** (Elo in a growing participant pool drifts), and the two snapshots gathered for this article disagree sharply. In a January 2026 snapshot Runway Gen-4.5 sat at #1 (~Elo 1247); by an April 2026 snapshot it had fallen to ~#10 (~1178), and the nominal #1 was **"HappyHorse-1.0"** — a model with **no identifiable team and no public API**, which by itself should make a reader sceptical of the leaderboard as procurement evidence. What the snapshots *do* agree on is the **cluster of genuine leaders**: Seedance 2.0, Kling 3.0, Veo 3.x, Hailuo, and Runway are consistently near the top (sources: [Artificial Analysis Video Arena](https://artificialanalysis.ai/video/leaderboard/text-to-video); [Jan 2026 snapshot](https://www.dualview.ai/blog/ai-tools/best-ai-video-models.html); [Apr 2026 snapshot](https://oakgen.ai/blog/best-ai-video-generators-april-2026-leaderboard)).

### Licence Traps

"Open" does not mean "free to use commercially". The specific traps to know:

- **LTX-Video** has the most confusing setup: the *repository code* is Apache-2.0, the *2B weights* (v0.9.5+) are **OpenRail-M** (commercial allowed with responsible-AI terms), but the flagship **13B weights** carry a **custom Lightricks licence** — so "LTX-Video is Apache" is wrong for the model most people want.
- **HunyuanVideo 1.5** is under the **Tencent Community Licence** — commercial use is allowed only **below 100M monthly active users** (a Llama-style cap), not a true permissive licence.
- **CogVideoX** uses a *modified* Apache licence with added restrictions; **Step-Video-T2V** and **SkyReels-V2** ship custom/under-specified licences that must be read before commercial deployment.
- **NVIDIA Cosmos** uses the NVIDIA Open Model License / OpenMDW with their own terms.
- Truly permissive (Apache 2.0 / MIT) and commercially safe: **Wan 2.1/2.2**, **Mochi 1**, **Open-Sora 2.0**, **Allegro**, **Pyramid Flow**.

### The Open-vs-Proprietary Gap

The widely-repeated claim that **"Wan 2.1 beat Sora on VBench"** (86.22% vs 84.28%) is true but **needs scoping**: it was measured at Wan 2.1's launch in **early 2025** and the "Sora" in that comparison is **Sora 1**, not the Sora 2 / Veo 3.1 / Seedance 2.0 generation that leads in 2026. Using a year-old VBench number to argue open models have caught up to the current frontier is a category error.

The honest 2026 picture: open models — **Wan 2.2** and **HunyuanVideo 1.5** especially — are **competitive for short social clips, B-roll, and I2V**, run on consumer hardware, and support fine-tuning (LoRA, ControlNet-style adapters) that closed APIs do not. They **lag the top proprietary models** on cinematic quality, reliable long-form coherence, and — most clearly — **native audio**, which only LTX-Video (13B) and the API-first Wan 2.5 offer at all on the open side. The gap is narrowing, but "narrowing" is a qualitative judgement from shifting leaderboards, not a measured constant — and it should be read that way (sources: [open-model landscape](https://insiderllm.com/guides/local-ai-video-generation/); [Wan review/VBench](https://chatforest.com/reviews/wan-2-1-alibaba-open-source-ai-video-generation/) — note this source is an AI-generated review, used only for figures corroborated elsewhere; [Open-Sora 2.0](https://github.com/hpcaitech/Open-Sora); [LTX-Video](https://github.com/Lightricks/LTX-Video); [SkyReels-V2](https://github.com/SkyworkAI/SkyReels-V2)).

## World Models and Interactive Video

The frontier beyond fixed-clip generation is the **world model**: a generator you can *navigate and act inside* in real time, where each user input conditions the next frame. These share architecture with video generators (DiTs, autoregressive frame prediction) but add **interactivity, controllability, and persistent state** — turning video generation into interactive simulation. They matter for gaming, film pre-visualisation, and — increasingly — as **training environments for embodied AI and robotics**.

- **Google DeepMind Genie 3** — reported as the first **real-time** interactive world model, generating navigable photorealistic environments from a text prompt at **~24 fps / 720p** with world consistency lasting minutes. Architecture (per the original Genie paper, 11B parameters) combines a **spatiotemporal video tokeniser + autoregressive dynamics model + latent action model**. DeepMind pairs it with the **SIMA** embodied agent to let agents practise tasks inside generated worlds — a closed training loop. (Genie 3's own technical paper was not published at the time of writing; details are inferred from the Genie 1 paper plus official blog posts.)
- **NVIDIA Cosmos** — **world foundation models for Physical AI**: generating synthetic training data and predicting future world states for robotics and autonomous systems, rather than creative clips. Cosmos *Predict/Transfer* models (7B+) are openly released under NVIDIA's Open Model / OpenMDW terms. A **"Cosmos 3" omnimodel** (adding robot action trajectories) was reportedly announced around June 2026, but this rests on **thin, partly AI-generated sourcing** and should be treated as preliminary until corroborated by NVIDIA's own documentation.
- **Decart Oasis** — an **open** real-time, Minecraft-style playable world model (~20 fps, next-frame prediction, no game engine); Decart raised US$300M at a ~$4B valuation (May 2026).
- **World Labs Marble** (Fei-Fei Li's company) — generates **editable 3D worlds** from text/images, exportable as **Gaussian splats or meshes** for 3D pipelines; Marble 1.1/1.1-Plus (April 2026) added automatic spatial expansion; public API since January 2026.
- **Odyssey Explorer** — **image-to-3D-world** for film/gaming, trained on real-world 360° captures (~10 min per world; Pixar co-founder Ed Catmull on the board).

The distinction from clip-generators is the key idea: a video model produces a fixed timeline; a world model maintains a **state** you can act on. This is where video generation, reinforcement learning, and 3D reconstruction (Gaussian splatting) are converging (sources: [Genie 3](https://deepmind.google/blog/genie-3-a-new-frontier-for-world-models/); [original Genie paper](https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.15391); [Oasis](https://oasis-model.github.io/); [Decart funding](https://siliconangle.com/2026/05/18/decart-raises-300m-ai-optimization-software-world-models/); [World Labs Marble](https://rits.shanghai.nyu.edu/ai/world-labs-releases-marble-1-1-auto-expanding-3d-world-generation/); [Odyssey Explorer](https://odyssey.ml/introducing-explorer)).

## Embedding Models and Evaluation

Video embeddings and evaluation are two faces of the same coin — both rest on contrastive video-text encoders, and both are where the LLM/embedding world meets video generation.

### Video-Text Embedding Models

| Model | Origin | Role |
|---|---|---|
| **CLIP / OpenCLIP** | OpenAI / LAION | The common ancestor; text+image dual encoder; conditions generation, powers CLIPSIM |
| **SigLIP / SigLIP 2** | Google | Sigmoid-loss CLIP successor; strong zero-shot; vision backbone for many VLMs |
| **ViCLIP** | OpenGVLab (InternVideo) | CLIP + spatiotemporal attention; trained on InternVid (~7M videos) |
| **InternVideo2** | OpenGVLab (ECCV 2024) | Video foundation model to 6B; masked modelling → contrastive → next-token; SOTA on 60+ tasks |
| **VideoPrism** | Google (ICML 2024) | Frozen general-purpose video encoder; SOTA on 31/33 understanding benchmarks |
| **LanguageBind** | PKU (ICLR 2024) | Binds video/audio/depth/thermal to a shared *language* space; video encoder in Video-LLaVA |
| **Twelve Labs Marengo, Gemini Embedding** | proprietary | Strong multimodal retrieval embeddings (API) |

These do triple duty: **(1) conditioning** generation (encoding the prompt/reference), **(2) retrieval** (finding similar clips in training data or libraries), and **(3) evaluation** (CLIPSIM measures text-frame alignment in CLIP's space). Because they are CLIP-descended, the **same encoders serve as vision backbones in multimodal LLMs** — the through-line that unifies this article's three companion topics (generation, understanding, embeddings).

One independent 2026 benchmark (Mixpeek) ranked proprietary embeddings (Twelve Labs Marengo 2.7, Google Gemini Embedding 2) above open ones (SigLIP 2, InternVideo2) for text-to-video retrieval — **but it used only 20 videos and 60 queries**, far too small to generalise, and InternVideo2's poor showing likely reflects an inference-config issue rather than true capability (it is SOTA on many academic benchmarks). Read it as a hint, not a verdict ([Mixpeek benchmark](https://mixpeek.com/blog/video-embedding-benchmark-2026)).

### Evaluation Benchmarks

- **VBench** (CVPR 2024 Spotlight) decomposes video-generation quality into **16 dimensions** (subject identity, motion smoothness, temporal flickering, spatial relationship, aesthetic quality, etc.) for fine-grained objective scoring. It is the de-facto standard open leaderboard.
- **VBench-2.0** (March 2025) extends to **18 sub-dimensions across 5 categories** — **Human Fidelity, Controllability, Creativity, Physics, and Commonsense** — targeting *intrinsic faithfulness* (does the video obey physics and common sense?) beyond surface quality.
- **FVD (Fréchet Video Distance)** measures the distance between real and generated video feature distributions using an **I3D** network. It remains widely reported but an **ICLR 2025 analysis showed it is unreliable as a standalone metric** — it underweights temporal distortion and inherits I3D's biases — and proposed **JEDi (JEPA Embedding Distance)** as a more reliable replacement.
- **CLIPSIM** — cosine similarity between generated frames and the text prompt in CLIP space; measures text adherence.
- **Movie Gen Bench** (Meta) — a large standardised T2V/audio benchmark releasing non-cherry-picked generations for fair comparison.
- **Artificial Analysis Video Arena** — the blind-vote human-preference arena (caveated above).

As with audio, these metrics **reuse the embedding models from the foundation section** — the embedding layer is conditioning mechanism, retrieval index, *and* yardstick at once (sources: [VBench](https://github.com/Vchitect/VBench); [VBench-2.0](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.21755); [JEDi / Beyond FVD](https://proceedings.iclr.cc/paper_files/paper/2025/hash/a57483b394a3654f4317051e4ce3b2b8-Abstract-Conference.html); [Movie Gen Bench](https://github.com/facebookresearch/MovieGenBench)).

## Hardware: What It Takes to Run Them

Unlike audio generation (where most models fit in <8 GB), video is **VRAM-hungry** — but 2026 quantisation and step-distillation have brought the leading open models within reach of consumer GPUs. The single biggest lever is **GGUF/FP8 quantisation plus model offloading**, which can cut a 14B model's footprint by half or more, at some quality cost. The standard tooling is **ComfyUI**, which has node support for every model below. The figures are approximate working numbers; quality at the most aggressive quantisation (Q3/Q4) is noticeably degraded and should be validated for your use case.

| Tier | Hardware | What runs here | Indicative speed |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Entry (8 GB)** | RTX 3050/4060, laptop GPUs | Wan 1.3B; **LTX-Video 2B (FP8)**; CogVideoX 2B | LTX: ~4–11 s for a 5 s clip on a 4090; minutes on entry cards |
| **12 GB** | RTX 3060/4070 | **Wan 14B (GGUF Q5, ~11 GB)**; CogVideoX 5B (offload) | Wan 14B Q5 on a 3060: ~10–15 min/clip |
| **16 GB** | RTX 4080, T4 | CogVideoX 5B (~16–18 GB tight); comfortable Wan/LTX | CogVideoX 5B: ~15 min on a 4090 |
| **24 GB** | RTX 3090/4090 | **Wan 2.2 14B (FP16)**; **HunyuanVideo 1.5 (~24 GB / 14 GB offload)**; Mochi 1 (~20 GB) | Hunyuan 1.5 *distilled*: ~75 s on a 4090 |
| **32 GB+ / data-centre** | RTX 5090, A100/H100 | Full-precision 14B; Step-Video 30B; training/fine-tuning; batch serving | H100 ≫ consumer for throughput |

**Speed-ups that matter** (all available as ComfyUI nodes or model variants):

- **Step distillation** — the biggest win. **CausVid** (CVPR 2025) converts a bidirectional video-diffusion model into a fast autoregressive one, enabling near-real-time/streaming generation; distilled variants (e.g. HunyuanVideo 1.5 distilled, distilled LTX) cut sampling from minutes to ~tens of seconds.
- **TeaCache** (timestep-embedding-aware caching) — training-free 1.5–2× speed-up on HunyuanVideo and LTX-Video.
- **SageAttention2++** — INT8/FP8 quantised attention kernels; reported +42% on Wan 2.1 14B image-to-video.
- **NVFP4/FP8** on RTX 40/50-series — vendor-announced large VRAM/speed gains (forward-looking; not yet independently benchmarked).

**Fine-tuning** is a real open-source advantage: **LoRA** adapters and **ControlNet-style** conditioning (LTX-Video's ICLoRA depth/pose/canny; Wan-Fun ControlNets; SkyReels start/end-frame) let teams customise subject, style, and motion in ways closed APIs do not expose.

**Cost economics.** Self-hosting an open model has **near-zero marginal cost** after hardware — an RTX 4090 (~$1,600) generates unlimited clips; amortised compute approaches **fractions of a cent per second** after a few hundred hours of use. Renting a cloud H100 (~$3–4/hr) breaks even against proprietary APIs ($0.03–0.75/s) somewhere around a few hundred hours of generation, depending on the API price point. Below that volume — and accounting for engineering, ops, and quality iteration — managed APIs are usually cheaper and far less effort (sources: [local video hardware guide](https://insiderllm.com/guides/local-ai-video-generation/); [CausVid](https://github.com/tianweiy/CausVid); [TeaCache](https://www.stablediffusiontutorials.com/2025/01/teacache.html); [SageAttention](https://v12h.substack.com/p/sageattention-sageattention2-sageattention2); [API landscape](https://ice-ice-bear.github.io/posts/2026-04-07-ai-video-api-landscape/)).

## Managed Service Equivalents (Hyperscalers)

For teams that prefer not to self-host, managed video generation is concentrated in three of the five hyperscalers; IBM and Oracle offer **GPU rental for self-hosting** rather than a managed video model.

- **GCP** — **Veo 3 / Veo 3.1 on Vertex AI**, the most capable hyperscaler offering, with native audio and SynthID watermarking. API pricing roughly **$0.15–0.36/s (Fast)** and **$0.40–0.75/s (Standard)**; also surfaced via Gemini app tiers (Google AI Pro $19.99/mo, Ultra $249.99/mo). Genie/world-model and Lyria audio also live in the Google stack.
- **Azure** — **Sora 2 via Azure AI Foundry** (Microsoft's OpenAI partnership), supporting text-to-video, image-to-video, and video-to-video (remix) with audio, on a per-second billing model (reported ~**$0.10/s**); **preview** status as of June 2026, so terms may change at GA.
- **AWS** — **Amazon Nova Reel on Bedrock** (first-party): **$0.08/s**, 720p/24 fps, up to ~2 min via multi-shot, **no audio**, US-East only, with C2PA watermarking. **Luma Ray2** is also available on Bedrock (pricing ~$0.25–0.50/s, low-confidence figure). - **Oracle** — no managed video-generation service; **OCI** is a competitive venue for **renting raw GPU** (≈$32/hr for 8× A100, ≈$80/hr for 8× H100, B200 from early 2026), often cheaper than equivalent AWS configs — useful for self-hosting any open model with full data control.

For self-hosting, all five clouds rent the GPUs in the [hardware tiers](#hardware-what-it-takes-to-run-them) above; a single 24 GB GPU covers most open-model workloads (sources: [Veo pricing](https://www.reelmation.com/blog/veo-3-pricing-ai-video-generation-cost.html); [Azure Sora 2 docs](https://learn.microsoft.com/en-us/azure/foundry/openai/concepts/video-generation); [Nova Reel](https://chatforest.com/reviews/amazon-nova-reel-aws-bedrock-video-generation/) (review corroborated against AWS pricing); [OCI GPU pricing](https://oraclelicensingexperts.com/blog/oracle-cloud-gpu-skus-pricing/)).

## Safety, Provenance, and Regulation

Realistic synthetic video is a dual-use technology, and 2026's frontier models increasingly ship with provenance and safety controls — though coverage is uneven and enforcement is nascent.

- **Watermarking & provenance.** **Google Veo** embeds **SynthID** (an invisible watermark) in every output; **Amazon Nova Reel** applies **C2PA** content credentials. **C2PA / Content Credentials** (the Adobe-led provenance standard) is the cross-industry direction, and Adobe Firefly leans on it for "commercially safe" claims. Provenance is not detection, however — watermarks can be stripped or degraded, and open models generally emit no provenance signal at all.
- **Regulation.** The **EU AI Act (Article 50)** requires that AI-generated or manipulated audiovisual content be **clearly labelled as such**, with transparency obligations phasing in through 2026. **China's deep-synthesis regulations** already mandate labelling and traceability for synthetic media — directly relevant given that the leading open and several proprietary models (Wan, Kling, Hunyuan, Seedance) originate from Chinese labs. Jurisdictions diverge, and a model that is compliant in one may not be in another.
- **Misuse and the deepfake link.** Photorealistic talking-head and image-to-video generation is exactly the capability behind non-consensual imagery, impersonation, and political manipulation. This article is the *capability* survey; the **threat model, offensive toolchain, and detection/defence** are covered in the companion articles [`deepfake-creation-models-and-accessibility.md`](deepfake-creation-models-and-accessibility.md) and [`facial-recognition-deepfake-impersonation-detection.md`](facial-recognition-deepfake-impersonation-detection.md).
- **Training-data provenance.** Most proprietary models do not disclose training data; **Adobe Firefly** (Adobe Stock + licensed) advertises the cleanest posture. Open models shift legal responsibility to whoever deploys them — the licence may permit use, but training-data provenance is rarely documented, and video-specific copyright litigation is an unsettled and fast-moving area.

## Areas of Uncertainty

- **Leaderboard volatility.** Artificial Analysis Video Arena Elo is **not comparable across snapshots**; the Jan→Apr 2026 reshuffle (Runway #1→#10) and a no-API "HappyHorse-1.0" topping the April list mean absolute ranks are unreliable. Use the leader *cluster*, not the ordinal positions.
- **Stale cross-model benchmarks.** The Wan 2.1 "beat Sora on VBench" figure is early-2025 and refers to Sora 1. There is **no single current VBench-2.0 table** ranking all 2026 frontier models that this review could verify.
- **Version reality.** Some cited versions (Wan 2.5/2.6/2.7, models beyond Kling 3.0 / Seedance 2.0) come from secondary or possibly unofficial sources; release dates and open-weight status per version are **lower-confidence**.
- **NVIDIA Cosmos 3 & Genie 3 internals.** Both rest partly on AI-generated blogs (chatforest.com) or unpublished papers; architecture and June-2026 release claims should be re-verified against primary vendor documentation.
- **Embedding rankings.** The only independent video-embedding benchmark found (Mixpeek) used 20 videos / 60 queries — too small to be definitive, with a likely-misconfigured InternVideo2 result.
- **VRAM/quality trade-off.** "Runs on 12 GB" via Q4 quantisation does not mean "runs *well*" — quality-at-quantisation was not systematically measured.
- **Pricing.** Per-second API prices change monthly and vary by resolution/mode/reseller; figures are indicative.

## Caveats and Limitations

- **Source mix.** Primary sources (arXiv papers, official GitHub/HuggingFace cards, vendor and hyperscaler docs) anchor the **technical foundation, architecture, licence, and managed-service** claims and are high-confidence. **Market, pricing, capability-comparison, and leaderboard** claims lean on commercial comparison blogs (credibility ~0.6–0.8) with promotional incentives; these were cross-checked where possible and should be re-verified before procurement.
- **AI-generated sources flagged.** `chatforest.com` is an AI-generated content site; it was used **only** for figures corroborated elsewhere (e.g. Wan version history) and is explicitly flagged where load-bearing (NVIDIA Cosmos 3).
- **"Open" ≠ free-to-use.** HunyuanVideo (≤100M MAU cap), LTX-Video 13B (custom Lightricks), CogVideoX (modified Apache), Step-Video / SkyReels (custom), and NVIDIA Cosmos (Open Model/OpenMDW) all carry conditions. Verify the exact licence — and training-data provenance — before commercial deployment.
- **Long-form coherence is unsolved.** Maintaining narrative and physical consistency beyond ~10–20 s remains the core open problem; "2-minute" and "infinite-length" claims describe *length*, not sustained coherence.
- **Geographic/language coverage.** Capability and ranking claims are English-source-centric despite Chinese labs dominating the open and much of the proprietary field; Chinese-language technical documentation and user reports were under-sampled.
- **Recency.** All figures reflect a **June 2026** snapshot of a field that changes monthly. Re-verify before relying on any specific number.
